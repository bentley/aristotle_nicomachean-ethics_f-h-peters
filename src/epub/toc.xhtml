  6. I: The End
    1. 1. In all he does man seeks some good as end or means
    2. 2. The end is the good; our subject is this and its science, Politics
    3. 3. Exactness not permitted by subject, nor to be expected by student, who needs experience and training
    4. 4. Men agree that the good is happiness, but differ as to what this is. We must reason from facts accepted without question by the man of trained character
    5. 5. The good cannot be pleasure, as some hold, nor honour, nor virtue
    6. 6. Various arguments to show against the Platonists that there cannot be one universal good: even if there were it would not help us here
    7. 7. The good is the final end, and happiness is this. To find it we ask, What is man’s function? Resulting definition of happiness
    8. 8. This view harmonizes various current views
    9. 9. Is happiness acquired, or the gift of Gods or chance?
    10. 10. Can no man be called happy during life?
    11. 11. Cannot the fortunes of survivors affect the dead?
    12. 12. Happiness as absolute end is above praise
    13. 13. Division of the faculties and resulting division of the virtues
  7. II: Moral Virtue
    1. 1. Moral virtue is acquired by the repetition of the corresponding acts
    2. 2. These acts must be such as reason prescribes; they cannot be defined exactly, but must be neither too much nor too little
    3. 3. Virtue is in various ways concerned with pleasure and pain
    4. 4. The conditions of virtuous action as distinct from artistic production
    5. 5. Virtue not an emotion, nor a faculty, but a trained faculty or habit
    6. 6. Viz. the habit of choosing the mean
    7. 7. This must be applied to the several virtues
    8. 8. The two vicious extremes are opposed to one another and to the intermediate virtue
    9. 9. The mean is hard to hit, and is a matter of perception not of reasoning
  8. III: The Will / The Several Moral Virtues and Vices
    1. 1. An act is involuntary when done (a) under compulsion, or (b) through ignorance: (a) means not originated by doer, (b) means through ignorance of the circumstances: voluntary, then, means originated with knowledge of circumstances
    2. 2. Purpose, a mode of will, means choice after deliberation
    3. 3. We deliberate on what we can do⁠–⁠not on ends, but means
    4. 4. We wish for the end, the real or apparent good
    5. 5. Virtue and vice are alike voluntary: our acts are our own; for we are punished for them: ignorance is no excuse when due to negligence: if this be our character, we have made it by repeated acts: even bodily vices are blamable when thus formed. We cannot plead that our notion of good depends on our nature; for (1) vice would still be as voluntary as virtue, (2) we help to make ourselves what we are

  10. V: Justice
    1. 1. Preliminary. Two senses of justice distinguished. Of justice (1) = obedience to law, = complete virtue
    2. 2. Of justice (2) = fairness, how related to justice (1). What is just in distribution distinguished from what is just in correction

  11. VI: The Intellectual Virtues
    1. 1. Must be studied because (a) reason prescribes the mean, (b) they are a part of human excellence. The intellect is (1) scientific, (2) calculative: we want the virtue of each
    2. 2. The function of the intellect, both in practice and speculation, is to attain truth
    3. 3. Of the five modes of attaining truth: (1) of demonstrative science of things unalterable
    4. 4. Of knowledge of things alterable, viz. (2) of art in wht we make
    5. 5. And (3) of prudence in what we do, the virtue of the calculative intellect
    6. 6. (4) Of intuitive reason as the basis of demonstrative science
    7. 7. (5) Of wisdom as the union of science and intuitive reason. Comparison of the two intellectual virtues, wisdom and prudence
    8. 8. Prudence compared with statesmanship and other forms of knowledge
